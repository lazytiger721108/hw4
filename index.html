Goal & User Research

Company Name: Wolfia

Tagline: "Get feedback on a mobile app by sharing a link"

For our start-up, we chose Wolfia from the Y-Combinator accelerator. It was founded by Fabien Devos and Naren Manoharan in 2022. The problem they were trying to solve was getting feedback to mobile app developers more efficiently and effectively through a shared link. Their model included feedback from Product Managers, Designers, and other Developers.

We decided the best way to achieve the goal of getting feedback through a shared link was through a web app with multiple “sides”. On the “Developer” side, developers would be able to upload their mobile apps and generate a link to send to various users for testing and feedback. They could also access a Feedback studio to ask for specific feedback and also see the feedback they have been given. On the “Reviewer” side, users would be able to use a variety of tools (code and non-code) to leave feedback on the app.

We generated ideas for our design by creating quick sketches exploring various possibilities for the website's interfaces. We focused on the pages enabling users to upload an app, view feedback on their app, and leave feedback on apps created by others.

In order to turn our sketches into wireframes, we focused on the principles of interaction: interaction, navigation, and hierarchy:

Interaction: To increase interaction, we incorporate lots of icons (that were both learnable and actionable) into our feedback studio. Additionally, based on Jacob Nielsen’s Rule of Thumb, we focused on three things to maximize interaction: consistency and standards, match between system and real world (or user’s mental models), and user control and freedom. Consistency and standards led us to minimize the colors we were using and also make sure t hat we included similar language, formatting, and features on various pages. For the match between system and real world, we tried to incorporate aspects of common applications (eg. drag and drop to upload), and think about what information a new user might need. Lastly, for user control and freedom, we wanted to make both the Reviewer Feedback and Developer Feedback as customizable and flexible as possible.

Navigation: We decided to use a side navigation bar. We also tried to group by topic (Project vs. Feedback on the sidebar) so that developers could easily find what they were looking for. We tried to test to make sure that users knew where they were and could hopefully figure out where they might want to go next.

Hierarchy: Used headings, different font weights, and indentation to show the hierarchy of different pages.

Critique, Feedback, and Modifications

Feedback

During a critique session, our peers pointed out some places where we could improve hierarchy and accessibility for users and developers alike.

Our solution

“Log in” or “Log In” buttons were inconsistent in their capitalization.

Buttons made consistent throughout the login page and also throughout the entire prototype (standardized button size, font, and color).

Side navigation bar could have a better hierarchy.

Improved the hierarchy in the side navigation bar through the use of font size, font weight, and indentation.

“New Feedback” and “Recent Feedback” on the hub page seemed too similar. Reconsider the headings and the organization of this page.

Changed “Recent Feedback” to “All Feedback” to remove ambiguity. Thought through the most important shortcuts.

(Peers were presented with several different color/contrast options for the Feedback page) For the Feedback page, pick the option with the dark background, and light icons with white space.

Implemented the high-contrast option, used consistent icons across pages to remove ambiguity.

Incorporate live analytics for specific questions ( flesh out the Dev Question Studio a little bit more).

Made a new screen to show the question studio, which allows developers to see existing responses to each question.

On the upload/App pages, show buttons more clearly (especially the customize feedback button) through colors or joining them in one bar.

Button colors and locations were modified to make their purpose more clear, linked related buttons by placing the functions related to sharing in the same container.

Suggestions

User 1: “The left-hand menu could be clearer. I was able to find things using the general layout of the design (which helps a user find their way). The interface is designed well enough for the average person to figure out, but it took me a while.”

User 2: Did not leave additional feedback.

User 3: “I think [the interface] is laid out very well. Even though there is not a lot of color in the site, I still like it. It was still very easy [to navigate] on the left hand side. I like the layout and it just helped things flow well.”

User 3: “It might be nice that once you have edited, something pops up like ‘changes saved’ so that you are assured that it does go through, no glitches, and it read what you did... Because sometimes technology happens. That might be nice to have there”

Analysis of User Testing

For the first task, finding feedback for App 1, two of the users found the task to be straightforward and very easy, while the third user struggled to navigate the site after getting caught on the “Reviewer” side of the interface as opposed to the “Developer” side. For the second and third tasks, all three of the users found the tasks to be straightforward and were able to complete them in a timely manner.

Based on user feedback, we would incorporate more action validation into the interface to keep users informed – this aligns with the interaction principle of visibility of system status. For example, we could incorporate pop-up windows, informative messages, or progress bars to show the user if their desired action has been completed. Additionally, we would increase the separation between the Reviewer and Developer sides of our prototype and the site itself by having the user link pop up in a new browser window. This would address the interaction principle of error prevention. We could also potentially add more color to the website to increase visual interest.

Conclusion
Conclusions

Persona and storyboarding, two effective tools for a designer, not only enable me to better understand how groups of users interact with a product, but also further my understanding of a product.

During this assignment, I learned a lot of lessons. First of all, I should clearly state my observation, which is different from interviews. Second, the storyboard is built based on the persona, so the storyboard must fit the persona exactly.

According to the feedback from reviewers, most of them think that our design is visually appealing and shows details about user feedback and how we addressed changes. But for our "minimalist style", some reviewers think that the interface is a bit monotone, which may be hard to identify key graphics and make the learning process harder for the admins. Also some reviewers are still confused about some of the function/button names.

If I have time, I will refactor my persona and storyboard to make my storyboard more suitable for my persona. Moreover, I will have more interview questions, such as users' views on a certain component, etc., so as to understand the user experience more comprehensively.
